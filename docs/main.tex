\begin{abstract}
    Social media platforms, like TikTok have become one of the dominant
    channels through which information, entertainment, and political and
    cultural trends propagate at scale. A defining feature of these
    platforms is the extreme skewness in attention: while the vast majority
    of uploaded videos receive little to no exposure, a small fraction
    achieve \textit{viral} reach, accumulating orders of magnitude more
    views within a short time window. This heavy-tailed distribution of
    engagement naturally raises the question of how to operationalize and
    predict “virality” in a principled way.

    This investigation explores the use of Machine Learning techniques to
    pin down a formal framework for the task of predicting the likelihood of
    a TikTok video going viral using both intrinsic (e.g. video content) and
    extrinsic features (e.g. video author).
\end{abstract}

\subsection*{Task definition}

The definition of virality (is\_viral) we produced is the target dimension we
optimize for. It is meant to capture outliers relative to any given account
stack-ranked in absolute terms with the goal of producing a signal that's useful
for any account size.

In order to recreate a mixed signal that fits the previous definition, we
compute two scores for each video, \textbf{Engagement} and \textbf{View
Velocity}.

\begin{align}
    \text{Engagement}_i &= \frac{\text{share\_count} \times w_0 + \text{save\_count} \times w_1 + \text{comment\_count} \times w_2 + \text{like\_count} \times w_3}{\text{play\_count}} \\
\end{align}

Where $Engagement$ is meant to represent a volume of "interactions" a video had
relative to its play count, making the score relative to the account size. As
shown in the following graph, its expected to assume that accounts with a larger
audience will most likely outperform its counterparts, making these videos seem
more "viral" in absolute measurements.

% TODO: Insert graph showing distribution of play count binned by account size.

What this signal is meant to capture instead is a degree of "engagement" that's
normalized to the audience size of any given account. To convey the idea, a
small account with a few hundred followers that produces a video that generates
thousands of interactions would have a comparable engagement to an acconut with
millions of followers that accumulated hundredths of thousands of interactions
instead.

\begin{align}
    \text{View Velocity}_i &= \frac{\text{play\_count}}{\text{delta\_hours}}
\end{align}

Where \textbf{View Velocity} normalizes the value of $play\_count$ by the total
amount of time a video has been live collecting interactions. A video with a
high "view velocity" is collecting interactions "faster" relative to the rest.

% TODO: Insert graph showing distribution of play count binned by delta hours.

We add together both measurements to produce a signal $S_i$ for each video that
we use to stack-rank them in absolute $S_i$ terms and consider the top $5\%$ as
"viral":

\begin{align}
    S_i &= \frac{\text{Engagement}_i}{\text{Engagement}_{max}} + \frac{\text{View Velocity}_i}{\text{View Velocity}_{max}} \\
    y_{\text{is\_viral}}^{(i)} &= 
    \begin{cases} 
      1 & \text{if } S_i \geq F_S^{-1} (0.95) \\
      0 & \text{Otherwise}
    \end{cases}
\end{align}

The reason we approached the concept of virality as described is rooted in
convenience and usefulness. There are just too many dynamic forces that could
influence the level of virality a video could reach. Many things like the
current social discourse, ongoing political and economic events, or just sheer
luck (being in the right place at the right time) could determine the success of
any given video.

Trying to capture such a broad range of "reasons" is likely near impossibility
limits either due to data or resource constraints.

Instead we try to condense a signal that's useful even for small accounts with
the available data. We're thinking about small businesses/ content creators
looking to amplify their social presence. Rolling out a video that's a couple of
folds more viral relative to their expected baseline is considered a win!

\subsection*{Model architecture}

The architecture implements a mixture of state-of-the-art ML processing
techniques for the different data modalities fed into the model; all these get
concatenated and passed through a late fusion module to finally produce 2
regression heads for the engagement and view velocity scores and 1
classification head for the virality class. We ultimately stitch them together
to compute a final weighted loss to have the different output targets contribute
in different quantities.

\subsubsection*{Data modalities}

\subsubsection*{Text input}

The text input is a concatenation of the different text dimensions in the
following form:

\begin{quote}
    "[CLS] [TITLE] ... [MUSIC] ... [CHALLENGES] ... [OBJECTS] ... [LOC] ..."
\end{quote}

That we populate with the example's data and feed this into a pretrained, frozen
\href{https://huggingface.co/distilbert/distilbert-base-uncased}{DistilBERT}
backbone to extract a 768-dimensional semantic representation from the [CLS]
token, capturing the semantics of all textual components from the example in a
compact representation.

\subsubsection*{Video input}

During the dataset composition step, the original videos were downsized and
downsampled to 224x224 pixels at 1 FPS to reduce processing requirements. From
that transformed version, we sample 16 frames that we feed into a pretrained,
frozen \href{https://huggingface.co/MCG-NJU/videomae-base}{VideoMAE} model to
encode the video into a spatial-temporal embedding space.

We then apply global average pooling across the output tokens to yield a
768-dimensional embedding that captures both visual features and temporal
dynamics.

\subsubsection*{Tabular input}

The tabular branch processes 19 heterogeneous features including author signals,
temporal metadata, and video quality scores. To handle the varying scales of
these inputs, we apply $log(1+x)$ scaling to high-variance author statistics and
sine/cosine cyclic encoding to temporal features like hour of day to preserve
periodic relationships. These are fed into a 2-layer MLP with layer
normalization and dropout, projecting them into a learnable 512-dimensional
space.

\subsubsection*{Late fusion}

In the late fusion module, we concatenate the 768-dimension text embedding,
the 768-dimension video embedding, and the 512-dimension tabular output into a
single 2048-dimensional vector. This combined representation is passed through a
shared fusion MLP, which maps the multimodal features into a unified latent
space of $d\_model=512$. This shared representation allows the model to learn
complex inter-modal correlations before branching into specialized tasks.

\subsubsection*{Multi-head targets}

We employ a multi-head architecture to simultaneously model continuous and
categorical virality signals. Each head progressively halves the upstream
$d\_model$ to reduce dimensionality while increasing specialization:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Engagement and velocity heads: Two regression heads predict the
    continuous engagement and view velocity scores.
    \item Classification head: A single head predicts the probability of
    virality, defined by the 95th percentile of the combined engagement and
    velocity distribution.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Weighted loss target}

The loss target is a composite of the different upstream heads, weighted to
prioritize the primary classification task while using the regression heads as
auxiliary signals:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Loss composition: We use Huber loss for the regression targets to
    remain robust against the extreme outliers common in viral distributions,
    and weighted BCE for the classification target.
    \item Weighting strategy: We set $\lambda_{cls}=0.7$ and
    $\lambda_{reg}=0.3$. This heavy weighting on classification ensures the
    model focuses on detecting the rare viral events (upweighted by a factor of
    $virality\_threshold / (1 - virality\_threshold)$ in the BCE loss), while
    the regression task provides a continuous "popularity" signal that
    stabilizes the shared latent space.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Experiments and results}

To evaluate the performance of our multimodal approach, we track a suite of
metrics that address both the regression of engagement signals and the
classification of rare viral events. Given the high degree of class imbalance in
our dataset (where viral videos constitute only the top 5\% of the
distribution), we prioritize metrics that are robust to sparsity.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Precision-Recall AUC (PR-AUC):} Our primary metric for the
    classification head. Unlike ROC-AUC, PR-AUC does not reward the model for
    correctly predicting the "non-viral" majority, providing a more honest view
    of the model's ability to identify viral content.
    \item \textbf{F1-Score:} Calculated at the 0.5 threshold to measure the
    harmonic mean of precision and recall.
    \item \textbf{ROC-AUC:} Used to measure the general separability of the
    classes across all potential thresholds.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Training Dynamics}

The training process spanned 15 epochs, utilizing a linear learning rate decay
starting from $5 \times 10^{-5}$. We observed a consistent reduction in
\texttt{eval\_loss} from 0.649 to 0.568 by epoch 15.

The final evaluation yields an \textbf{ROC-AUC of 0.7875}, indicating that the
model has developed a strong capability to distinguish between viral and
non-viral content. However, the \textbf{PR-AUC of 0.1805} and
\textbf{F1-score of 0.2452} highlight the inherent difficulty of the task.

While the ROC-AUC is promising, the low PR-AUC suggests that while the model
ranks viral videos higher than non-viral ones, its absolute confidence in these
predictions remains low. This is likely due to:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Intrinsic stochasticity:} Virality on TikTok is often driven
    by external algorithmic amplification or "luck" (the cold-start problem)
    that cannot be fully captured by content features alone.
    \item \textbf{Information asymmetry:} Despite multimodal fusion, the frozen
    encoders (DistilBERT and VideoMAE) may be missing platform-specific nuances
    like trending audio or specific visual tropes that significantly impact
    short-form video success.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Next steps}

While the current model establishes a strong baseline for late-fusion multimodal
learning, the discrepancy between ROC-AUC and PR-AUC suggests that the model is
struggling with the extreme sparsity of viral signals. We propose the following
directions for future research:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Run \textbf{ablation experiments} on the input features to surface
    relevance (e.g. feeding everything but the video input, and viceversa).
    \item An interesting follow-up we pondered on involves incorporating an
    auxiliary regression task to predict the continuous growth size of
    engagement overtime using a \textbf{Self-Exciting Hawkes Process} regression
    module.
\end{itemize}

Our investigation demonstrates that while intrinsic content features provide a
signal for virality, the task remains highly stochastic. By leveraging a
multi-task learning framework with weighted losses, we effectively stabilized
the training of a high-dimensional multimodal model, achieving a respectable
ROC-AUC that serves as a foundation for future algorithmic discovery in social
media dynamics.
